# Glossary of Key Terms

!!! info "Page status"
    **Level:** Beginner &nbsp;·&nbsp; **Status:** :material-circle: Drafted &nbsp;·&nbsp; **Last reviewed:** 2026-06-14

> **Purpose:** A single, plain-language reference for the terms used across this wiki, so everyone speaks the same language.

## In one sentence

This is the team's shared dictionary — quick, plain-English definitions of the microbiome and sequencing terms you will hear every day.

## Key points

- Definitions run from everyday concepts (microbiome, gut health) to technical ones (ASV, CpG site).
- Terms are listed **alphabetically** for fast lookup.
- Most entries are general definitions; a few carry a citation where a specific source is directly relevant.
- When in doubt about wording in a customer conversation, default to the phrasing here.

## The detail

**Alpha diversity** — A measure of how varied the microbial community is *within* a single sample (how many different types, and how evenly balanced).

**Amplicon** — A specific stretch of DNA that has been copied (amplified) many times so it can be sequenced; in microbiome work this is often part of the 16S rRNA gene.

**ASV (Amplicon Sequence Variant)** — A high-resolution way of grouping sequencing reads that distinguishes organisms down to single-nucleotide differences, rather than lumping them into broader clusters.[^knight2018]

**Beta diversity** — A measure of how *different* two or more samples are from each other in their microbial makeup.

**Biomarker** — A measurable biological signal (such as a molecule or a microbial pattern) used to indicate a state of health or disease.

**CpG site** — A spot in DNA where a cytosine (C) sits directly next to a guanine (G); these sites are common targets of DNA methylation.

**DNA methylation** — The addition of a methyl group to cytosine (forming 5-methylcytosine), a chemical mark that helps regulate whether genes are switched on or off.[^moore2012]

**Dysbiosis** — An unhealthy imbalance in the microbial community, associated with a range of intestinal and whole-body conditions.

**Epigenetics** — The study of changes in gene activity that do not alter the underlying DNA sequence, such as chemical marks that turn genes up or down.

**Epigenome** — The full set of epigenetic marks (like methylation) across all of an organism's DNA.

**Human Genome Project (HGP)** — The landmark international research effort (1990–2003) that produced the first essentially complete map of the roughly 3 billion DNA "letters" (base pairs) that make up a human genome. Think of it as drawing the first full reference map of human DNA. Its completion in 2003 gave science a shared reference and kick-started the technology race that has driven sequencing costs down dramatically ever since — which is precisely what makes today's fast, affordable microbiome and methylation testing possible.[^goodwin2016]

**16S rRNA** — A gene present in all bacteria that varies just enough between species to act as a "barcode" for identifying which bacteria are present.

**Alpha vs beta diversity** — Remember it as: alpha = diversity *within* one sample; beta = differences *between* samples.

**Metagenome** — All the genetic material from a microbial community, sequenced and read together.

**Microbiome** — The community of microbes in a given environment (such as the gut) together with their collective genes.

**Microbiota** — The living microorganisms themselves — bacteria, archaea, viruses, fungi, and other single-celled organisms.

**NGS (Next-Generation Sequencing)** — A family of modern, high-throughput sequencing technologies that read DNA quickly and cheaply; the cost of sequencing has fallen dramatically since the Human Genome Project.[^goodwin2016]

**OTU (Operational Taxonomic Unit)** — An older way of grouping similar sequencing reads into clusters (typically at ~97% similarity) to approximate a species; largely superseded by ASVs.[^knight2018]

**Proteomics** — The large-scale study of all the proteins in a sample, complementing DNA- and RNA-based methods.

**Short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs)** — Small molecules (such as acetate, propionate, and butyrate) produced when gut microbes ferment dietary fibre; they influence gut, immune, and even brain function.

**Shotgun metagenomics** — A sequencing approach that reads all the DNA in a sample at once, allowing analysis of community *function* and strain-level detail rather than just identity.

**Species** — A fundamental unit of biological classification; a group of closely related organisms.

**Strain** — A specific variant within a species; two strains of the same bacterial species can behave very differently.

**Taxonomy** — The system of naming and classifying organisms into ranks (such as genus, family, order, and phylum).

## Common questions

**"What's the difference between OTU and ASV?"**
Both group sequencing reads, but ASVs resolve organisms down to single-nucleotide differences, while OTUs cluster reads at a similarity threshold (often ~97%). ASVs are the more modern, higher-resolution approach.[^knight2018]

**"Why does sequencing keep getting cheaper?"**
Next-generation sequencing technologies have driven the cost down dramatically since the era of the Human Genome Project, which is what makes large-scale microbiome testing practical today.[^goodwin2016]
