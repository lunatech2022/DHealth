# DNA Methylation

!!! info "Page status"
    **Level:** Intermediate &nbsp;·&nbsp; **Status:** :material-circle: Drafted &nbsp;·&nbsp; **Last reviewed:** 2026-06-14

> **Purpose:** Explain what DNA methylation is at a chemical level, how it controls genes, and why its dysregulation matters for health.

## In one sentence

DNA methylation is a chemical tag added to DNA that usually turns a gene down or off, and it is one of the body's most important tools for controlling gene activity.

## Key points

- Methylation adds a methyl group to the C5 position of cytosine, forming 5-methylcytosine, most often at "CpG" sites.[^moore2012]
- It regulates genes by recruiting repressive proteins or by blocking transcription factors from binding.[^moore2012]
- Methylation patterns are dynamic, changing as cells develop and specialize.[^moore2012]
- Environment and lifestyle can modulate methylation over time.[^moore2012]
- Abnormal methylation is linked to disease, including cancer.[^moore2012][^dawson2012]

## The detail

At the chemical level, DNA methylation is the transfer of a methyl group onto the **C5 position of cytosine**, one of the four DNA building blocks, producing a modified base called **5-methylcytosine**.[^moore2012] In mammals this happens predominantly where a cytosine sits next to a guanine, a pairing called a **CpG site**. Clusters of these sites, often near the start of genes, are key control points for whether a gene is read.

Methylation influences gene activity in two main ways. First, the methyl tag can **recruit repressive proteins** that bind methylated DNA and shut the local region down. Second, it can **physically block transcription factors**, the proteins that switch genes on, from reaching their target sequences.[^moore2012] In both cases the practical effect is the same: a methylated gene is typically quieted, while an unmethylated one is more available to be expressed.

| Aspect | What happens |
| --- | --- |
| Chemical change | Methyl group added to C5 of cytosine → 5-methylcytosine[^moore2012] |
| Where | Mostly at CpG sites[^moore2012] |
| Effect on genes | Usually represses (turns down/off)[^moore2012] |
| Two mechanisms | Recruits repressive proteins; blocks transcription factors[^moore2012] |
| Nature | Dynamic, reversible, responsive to environment[^moore2012] |

Crucially, methylation is not static. Patterns are actively established and erased through **de novo methylation and demethylation during development**, allowing cells to take on specialized identities.[^moore2012] Beyond development, neuronal activity and environmental stimuli can modulate methylation, meaning lifestyle and surroundings leave measurable marks on the methylome.[^moore2012]

When methylation goes wrong, through mutations or environmental risk factors, the consequences can be serious. Altered methylation is associated with disease,[^moore2012] and disrupted epigenetic control, including DNA methylation, is a recognized driver of cancer.[^dawson2012]

!!! tip "So what?"
    Because methylation is both meaningful and measurable, it gives us a readable record of how genes have been regulated. It reflects environment and aging, it changes when health changes, and abnormal patterns can flag disease, which is exactly why methylation is such a powerful basis for diagnostics.

## Why it matters for Dayhoff / DHealth

DNA methylation is the core signal behind methylation-based testing. When the team talks about reading the epigenome, this is the mark being read. Knowing that methylation typically silences genes, sits at CpG sites, and shifts with environment and disease lets reps explain what a methylation test actually detects and why the result is biologically meaningful.

## Common questions

**Does methylation always switch a gene off?**
Usually it turns genes down or off, but the effect depends on where it occurs. The general rule of "methylation represses" is a good starting point.[^moore2012]

**What is a CpG site?**
It is a spot where a cytosine is followed by a guanine in the DNA. These are the locations where methylation most commonly occurs in humans.[^moore2012]

**Can my methylation change?**
Yes. Methylation is dynamic and can be modulated by environment and activity over a lifetime.[^moore2012]
