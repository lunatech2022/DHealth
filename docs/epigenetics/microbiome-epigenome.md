# Microbiome–Epigenome Interactions

!!! info "Page status"
    **Level:** Advanced &nbsp;·&nbsp; **Status:** :material-circle: Drafted &nbsp;·&nbsp; **Last reviewed:** 2026-06-14

> **Purpose:** Explain how gut microbes shape host epigenetics through their metabolites, and why this connects the microbiome to broader health.

## In one sentence

The gut microbiome influences how our own genes are regulated, largely through the metabolites it produces, linking what lives in the gut to host epigenetics and health.

## Key points

- Gut microbes ferment dietary fiber into short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs): butyrate, acetate, and propionate.[^alsharairi2023]
- These and other metabolites can influence host DNA methylation and histone acetylation.[^caspani2019]
- SCFAs help maintain gut barrier integrity and reduce inflammation via epigenetic modifications.[^alsharairi2023]
- Microbial metabolites act on the gut-brain axis, including through epigenetic regulation.[^caspani2019]
- The field is moving toward cause-and-effect understanding using multi-omics.[^fan2020]

!!! abstract "In simple terms"
    The bacteria living in your gut don't just digest food; they also send out chemical messages that reach your own cells. Think of them as tiny tenants who leave notes that can flip your genes on or off. When they break down fibre, they make helpful compounds that calm inflammation, strengthen your gut lining, and even influence your brain through these gene-level signals. So a healthy gut community isn't only about digestion; it's quietly helping tune how your body runs.

## The detail

The microbiome does more than digest food; it sends chemical signals that reach into our cells and influence how our genes are regulated. The clearest example is the family of **short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs)**, produced when anaerobic gut bacteria ferment dietary fiber. The major SCFAs are **butyrate, acetate, and propionate**, and they help maintain gut homeostasis, support barrier integrity, and reduce inflammation, importantly, doing so via epigenetic modifications including DNA and histone methylation.[^alsharairi2023]

The mechanism is striking: microbial metabolites can directly touch the host epigenome. Gut microbial metabolites, including SCFAs, indoles, and bile acids, act on the **microbiota-gut-brain axis** in part through **epigenetic regulation of histone acetylation and DNA methylation**.[^caspani2019] In other words, the chemical output of our gut bacteria can shape which of our own genes are switched on or off, particularly in pathways relevant to brain and immune function.

| Metabolite class | Source | Epigenetic / health link |
| --- | --- | --- |
| SCFAs (butyrate, acetate, propionate) | Microbial fermentation of fiber | Barrier integrity, anti-inflammatory, DNA/histone methylation[^alsharairi2023] |
| SCFAs, indoles, bile acids | Gut microbiota | Histone acetylation, DNA methylation on gut-brain axis[^caspani2019] |

This connects the microbiome to a wide range of health domains. Through the gut-brain axis, microbial metabolites and their epigenetic effects are relevant to neurological and mood-related processes,[^caspani2019] while their roles in barrier function and inflammation tie them to metabolic and gut health.[^alsharairi2023] The gut microbiota is broadly implicated in metabolic health and disease.[^fan2020]

The scientific frontier is **causation**. Rather than only cataloguing which microbes are present, the field is increasingly combining **metagenomics with metabolomics** and other layers to move toward cause-and-effect understanding of how the microbiome shapes host biology.[^fan2020] This multi-omics direction is where microbiome science and epigenetics most powerfully meet.

!!! tip "So what?"
    The microbiome is not just a passenger; its metabolites actively tune our epigenome, influencing inflammation, gut integrity, and even the gut-brain axis.[^alsharairi2023][^caspani2019] That means supporting a healthy microbiome may have benefits that reach well beyond digestion, and it makes the microbiome a meaningful, measurable lever for health.

## Why it matters for Dayhoff / DHealth

This page ties together the two pillars of our science: the microbiome and the epigenome. It lets the team explain why measuring both is more powerful than either alone, and why a multi-omics approach is the direction of the field.[^fan2020] For customers, it reframes the microbiome as an active influence on host gene regulation, not just a measure of gut bacteria.

## Common questions

**How can gut bacteria affect my own genes?**
Through the metabolites they make. SCFAs and other microbial products can influence host DNA methylation and histone modifications.[^alsharairi2023][^caspani2019]

**What are SCFAs and why do they matter?**
Short-chain fatty acids (butyrate, acetate, propionate) are produced when gut microbes ferment fiber. They support the gut barrier, reduce inflammation, and act through epigenetic modifications.[^alsharairi2023]

**Is the microbiome really linked to the brain?**
Microbial metabolites act on the gut-brain axis, including through epigenetic regulation, connecting gut microbes to brain-related processes.[^caspani2019]
