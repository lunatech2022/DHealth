# How to Read a Microbiome Report

!!! info "Page status"
    **Level:** Beginner &nbsp;·&nbsp; **Status:** :material-circle: Drafted &nbsp;·&nbsp; **Last reviewed:** 2026-06-14

> **Purpose:** Walk through a microbiome report section by section in plain language, so anyone can explain it confidently to a customer.

## In one sentence

A microbiome report is a snapshot of which microbes live in a sample and in what proportions, read section by section rather than as a single pass/fail score.

## Key points

- A report usually has three core sections: a **taxa list** (who is there), **diversity** (how varied the community is), and **relative abundance** (the proportions of each microbe).
- Some reports also include **flagged findings** — microbes or patterns worth a closer look.
- There is **no single "ideal" microbiome**. Healthy profiles vary widely between people.
- Numbers are **proportions**, not headcounts, so they always add up to a whole.
- Context matters: age, diet, geography, and recent medication all shape a normal result.

## The detail

Start at the top. Most reports open with a **taxa list** — the names of the bacteria (and sometimes other microbes) detected in the sample. Names are organised in a hierarchy, from broad groups down to narrower ones. A reader does not need to memorise these; the point is simply that the lab has identified *who* is present.

Next comes **diversity**. This is a measure of how many different types of microbe are present and how evenly they are balanced. As a general rule, a varied, balanced community is often associated with resilience, but diversity is a research signal rather than a verdict on any one person.

The third core section is **relative abundance** — the proportion each microbe makes up of the whole community. Because these are proportions, they are expressed as percentages that sum to 100%. If one group rises, others must fall, so a single high or low number is best read in the context of the whole profile.

Finally, some reports highlight **flagged findings**: a microbe present in an unusual proportion, or a pattern that researchers have studied in connection with health. Flags are prompts for conversation, not diagnoses.

The most important framing for customers is that there is **no universal "perfect" gut profile**. The composition of a healthy microbiome shifts across age, environment, diet, and health status, and what looks typical for one person may differ for another.[^rinninella2019] Equally, how a sample is collected, sequenced, and analysed all influence the result, which is why following consistent best practices matters when comparing reports.[^knight2018]

!!! tip "So what?"
    When a customer asks "is my result good?", reframe it. A report describes a community at one moment in time. The useful questions are about patterns and trends, not a single score.

## Why it matters for Dayhoff / DHealth

Reading a report well is the foundation of every customer conversation. Sales and support staff who can calmly walk through each section — and who lead with "there is no single ideal profile" — build trust and prevent the anxiety that comes from customers grading themselves against an imaginary perfect result. It also sets up the right expectations for repeat testing, where trends over time are far more meaningful than any one snapshot.

## Common questions

- **"What's a normal result?"** Normal varies from person to person; the report describes a community, not a grade.
- **"Why don't the numbers add up to a count of bacteria?"** They are proportions of the whole, so they sum to 100%.
- **"Should I worry about a flagged finding?"** Flags are points of interest for discussion, not diagnoses.
