# Taxonomy & Relative Abundance

!!! info "Page status"
    **Level:** Intermediate &nbsp;·&nbsp; **Status:** :material-circle: Drafted &nbsp;·&nbsp; **Last reviewed:** 2026-06-14

> **Purpose:** Explain how microbes are classified into ranks and what "relative abundance" really means — including why these numbers are proportions, not headcounts.

## In one sentence

Taxonomy is the naming system that sorts microbes from broad groups down to narrow ones, and relative abundance is the proportion each microbe makes up of the whole community.

## Key points

- Microbes are classified in **ranks**: phylum (broad) → family → genus → species (narrow).
- Reports often resolve confidently to **genus**, with species or strain being harder to pin down.
- **Relative abundance** is a *proportion* of the community, not a count of cells.
- Because proportions sum to 100%, the data are **compositional**: if one goes up, others go down.
- Compositional data must be read as a whole, not as independent numbers.

## The detail

Every microbe sits in a nested classification. From broad to narrow, the common ranks are **phylum**, then **class**, **order**, **family**, **genus**, and finally **species** (and sometimes **strain**). A useful analogy is geography: a phylum is like a continent, a family like a country, a genus like a city, and a species like a neighbourhood. Reports frequently report results most confidently at the **genus** level, because resolving all the way to species or strain depends heavily on the sequencing method and reference data available.[^johnson2019] This is why a report may say a genus is present without naming the exact species — that is expected, not a gap.

**Relative abundance** is the second key idea. Each microbe is reported as the *share* it makes up of the total community, usually a percentage. It is not a count of how many cells are present. Because every share is part of the same whole, the percentages add up to 100%. Data of this kind are called **compositional**.

The compositional nature has an important consequence: the numbers are **relative, not absolute**. If one group's share rises, the shares of others must fall to keep the total at 100% — even if the actual amount of those others did not change. So a single high or low percentage should never be read in isolation; it only makes sense relative to the rest of the profile. Sound analysis depends on consistent best practices precisely because these compositional effects can otherwise be misread.[^knight2018]

This also reinforces a recurring theme: there is no single "ideal" set of abundance values. Healthy profiles differ between people and shift with age, diet, environment, and health, so the same percentage can be perfectly normal for one person and unusual for another.[^rinninella2019]

!!! tip "So what?"
    Abundance numbers are proportions, not counts. When a customer fixates on one percentage, zoom out — the figure only means something relative to the whole community.

## Why it matters for Dayhoff / DHealth

Taxonomy and abundance are where customers most often misinterpret a report — reading a percentage as a headcount, or treating one genus's number as a stand-alone score. Staff who can explain ranks plainly and stress the compositional caveat ("relative, not absolute") prevent these errors, protect credibility, and keep conversations grounded in the whole profile rather than a single line.

## Common questions

- **"Does 5% mean five out of a hundred bacteria?"** It means that microbe is 5% of the community's *share*, not a literal count.
- **"Why didn't you name the exact species?"** Confident identification often stops at genus; resolving species or strain depends on the method.
- **"One number went up — is that bad?"** Because shares sum to 100%, one rising means others fell. Read it as part of the whole.
