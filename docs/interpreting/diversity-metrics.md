# Diversity Metrics (Alpha & Beta)

!!! info "Page status"
    **Level:** Intermediate &nbsp;·&nbsp; **Status:** :material-circle: Drafted &nbsp;·&nbsp; **Last reviewed:** 2026-06-14

> **Purpose:** Explain alpha and beta diversity in plain terms, why diversity is studied, and how it varies between people.

## In one sentence

Alpha diversity describes how varied the community is *within* one sample, while beta diversity describes how *different* two or more samples are from each other.

## Key points

- **Alpha diversity** = variety inside a single sample (how many types, how evenly balanced).
- **Beta diversity** = the difference *between* samples or groups of people.
- Diversity is studied because a varied, balanced community is often linked to resilience in research.
- Diversity is **not a grade**: it varies naturally by age and geography.
- Altered diversity has been observed in some conditions, such as early frailty.

## The detail

Think of a garden. **Alpha diversity** asks: how many kinds of plant are in *this* garden, and are they evenly spread or dominated by one species? In microbiome terms, it captures both the number of different microbes (richness) and how balanced their proportions are (evenness) within a single sample.

**Beta diversity** asks a different question: how different is *this* garden from the one next door? It compares whole communities against each other, which is how researchers tell, for example, whether two groups of people have distinct microbiome profiles. Because measuring and comparing communities is sensitive to method, consistent best practices in sampling and analysis are essential for diversity comparisons to be meaningful.[^knight2018]

Why is diversity studied at all? Across many studies, a richer and more balanced community is generally associated with a stable, resilient ecosystem, while reduced diversity sometimes appears alongside particular health states. It is important to stress this is a research-level signal, not a personal verdict.

Diversity also varies naturally. Large studies show that gut microbiome composition and diversity differ markedly **across age and geography** — an infant, an adult, and an elderly person, or people on different continents and diets, can all show very different but entirely normal profiles.[^yatsunenko2012] This is part of why there is no single "ideal" diversity number that fits everyone; the healthy range itself shifts with age, environment, diet, and health.[^rinninella2019]

That said, **altered diversity** has been documented in specific contexts. For example, distinct gut microbiota signatures have been associated with **early frailty**, illustrating how diversity patterns can track with health states under study.[^frailty2016] The takeaway is nuance: diversity is a useful lens, but it is interpreted in context rather than as a stand-alone score.

!!! tip "So what?"
    "Higher diversity is always better" is an oversimplification. Diversity is a research signal that varies by age, geography, and life stage — interpret it as a pattern, not a pass mark.

## Why it matters for Dayhoff / DHealth

Diversity metrics are among the most asked-about numbers on a report, and also the most misunderstood. Staff who can explain alpha vs beta clearly — and who set the expectation that diversity varies naturally by age and geography — help customers avoid over-reading a single figure. This builds credibility and supports the case for tracking trends over time rather than chasing one "high" number.

## Common questions

- **"Is higher diversity always better?"** Not strictly. It is a useful signal, but normal varies by age, geography, and life stage.
- **"Why is alpha diversity different from beta?"** Alpha is variety within one sample; beta is the difference between samples.
- **"Can diversity change?"** Yes — it shifts with age, environment, and health, which is why trends matter more than one reading.
