# Functional & Pathway Analysis

!!! info "Page status"
    **Level:** Advanced &nbsp;·&nbsp; **Status:** :material-circle: Drafted &nbsp;·&nbsp; **Last reviewed:** 2026-06-14

> **Purpose:** Move beyond "who is there" to "what they do" — functional profiling, pathways, short-chain fatty acids, microbial metabolites, and meta-omics.

## In one sentence

Functional analysis shifts the question from which microbes are present to what those microbes are capable of doing — the genes, pathways, and metabolites that shape how the community functions.

## Key points

- Taxonomy tells you **who** is present; functional analysis tells you **what they can do**.
- **Functional/pathway profiling** maps the community's genes to metabolic capabilities.
- **Short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs)** are key microbial products studied for their roles in health.
- The microbiome produces many **metabolites**, some beneficial and some less so.
- **Meta-omics** (metagenomics, metatranscriptomics, metabolomics) connect genes to activity.

!!! abstract "In simple terms"
    Listing which microbes live in your gut is like reading the names on a team roster; it tells you who's there but not what jobs they do. Functional analysis looks at the actual work: which chemicals they make and which tasks they can perform. That's why two people with completely different microbes can still be equally healthy, because different players can cover the same job. Scientists figure this out by reading the microbes' genes and the molecules they produce.

## The detail

A taxa list answers *who is there*. Functional analysis answers *what they are doing*, and the two can diverge: different microbes can perform the same job, so two people with different taxa lists may share similar functional capacity. To read function, researchers move beyond marker-gene surveys toward **shotgun metagenomics**, which sequences all the DNA in a sample and so captures the gene content — the functional potential — of the whole community.[^quince2017]

From that gene content, analysts build **pathway profiles**: maps of which metabolic routes the community is equipped to run. A flagship example is the production of **short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs)** such as butyrate, acetate, and propionate, which are made when gut microbes ferment dietary fibre. SCFAs are among the most studied microbial products because of their roles in gut and metabolic health, and they illustrate why *function* can matter as much as *identity*.[^alsharairi2023]

SCFAs are only part of a much larger picture. The microbiome generates a wide range of **metabolites** that interact with the host. Some have been studied in relation to mood and the gut–brain axis, where microbial metabolites have been investigated in the context of depression.[^caspani2019] Others can be less benign: for instance, certain gut bacteria can produce hydrogen sulfide, and the enzymatic basis for this has been characterised in *Bilophila wadsworthia*.[^bilophila2019] Function, in other words, spans a spectrum.

Capturing all of this is the domain of **meta-omics** — an umbrella for layered approaches: metagenomics (the genes present), metatranscriptomics (which genes are switched on), metaproteomics, and metabolomics (the molecules actually made). Combining these meta-omics layers is how the field moves from cataloguing potential toward understanding real, translational activity in the community.[^metaomics2019]

!!! tip "So what?"
    Two people can have different microbes yet similar functions. When a customer asks what their microbes *do*, that question is answered by pathways and metabolites — not by the taxa list alone.

## Why it matters for Dayhoff / DHealth

Functional analysis is where the science gets genuinely differentiating. Customers increasingly want to know not just *who* lives in their gut but *what it means*, and SCFAs and other metabolites are the bridge to that conversation. Staff who can explain the move from taxonomy to function — and name meta-omics as the toolkit behind it — position DHealth as scientifically credible and forward-looking, while being careful to frame these as research-grounded capabilities rather than diagnoses.

## Common questions

- **"If two people have different bacteria, can they be equally healthy?"** Yes — different microbes can perform the same functions.
- **"What are SCFAs?"** Short-chain fatty acids, made when gut microbes ferment fibre; widely studied in gut and metabolic health.
- **"What is meta-omics?"** A family of methods that read genes, their activity, and the molecules produced, to connect identity with function.
