# For Practitioners: GPs & Naturopaths

!!! info "Page status"
    **Level:** Intermediate &nbsp;·&nbsp; **Status:** :material-circle: Drafted &nbsp;·&nbsp; **Last reviewed:** 2026-06-14

> **Purpose:** Explain why microbiome, proteomic-blood, and methylation testing are relevant to different practitioner types, and give practical, honest ways to fold them into a care plan.

!!! warning "Important — scope of these tests"
    These tests are **informational and for wellness/research context**. They are **not a diagnostic device** and do not diagnose, treat, or cure disease. They are an **adjunct** to professional judgment, not a replacement. Any medical decision — diagnosis, prescribing, or medication dosing (including GLP-1) — remains with the qualified clinician.

## In one sentence

The microbiome and methylation are **modifiable, trackable biomarkers**, which makes them a natural fit for practitioners who want to measure a baseline, intervene with lifestyle, and show change over time.[^fan2020][^moore2012]

## Key points

- For **GPs**, the value is a functional layer that standard panels do not capture, plus an objective way to monitor lifestyle response — as an adjunct, not a diagnosis.
- For **naturopaths**, the tests give objective, repeatable evidence for the root-cause, lifestyle-first interventions they already use.
- The shared workflow is a loop: **baseline → interpret → intervene → re-measure**.
- Anything medical (diagnosis, prescriptions, GLP-1 dosing) is routed to the patient's physician.

!!! abstract "In simple terms"
    Most tests a doctor runs are a one-time snapshot of a fixed thing. The microbiome and methylation are different: they change when someone changes their diet, sleep, and stress. That makes them ideal for "let's measure where you are, make some changes, and check again" — which is exactly how good lifestyle care already works.

## For GPs / family physicians

- **A functional layer beyond standard panels.** A standard metabolic panel, CBC, or lipid panel measures a fixed set of analytes. Microbiome shotgun sequencing adds *functional* information — which organisms are present at strain level and what they can do (metabolism, inflammatory potential).[^quince2017][^johnson2019][^metaomics2019]
- **A modifiable monitor.** Because microbiome and methylation shift with lifestyle, they are useful for **tracking response** to diet, sleep, and other changes between visits, rather than a one-and-done genetic result.[^fan2020][^moore2012]
- **Relevant to today's high-volume concerns.** Metabolic health and GLP-1 use are common in practice; gut–microbiota and GLP-1 interact, and reviews stress individualised management — so an information layer on metabolic and nutritional status can support (not direct) the prescriber's plan.[^glp1micro2023]
- **Honest framing.** Position results as an **adjunct** that informs the clinical conversation. They do not replace examination, history, or diagnostics.

## For naturopaths / integrative practitioners

- **Fits a root-cause, lifestyle-first model.** The tests give objective biomarkers for the interventions naturopaths already favour — diet and fibre, sleep, stress, targeted nutrition.[^scfa2023][^fan2020]
- **Concrete, trackable targets.** Microbial diversity, short-chain-fatty-acid–producing capacity, and biological-age markers turn "eat better and rest more" into measurable goals that can be re-checked.[^frailty2016][^scfa2023][^microbiomeage2025]
- **Demonstrates progress.** Re-testing after an intervention period provides evidence that the plan is moving biomarkers in the right direction — valuable for client motivation and retention.

## Incorporating into a care plan

A simple, defensible five-step loop:

1. **Baseline.** Choose the test(s) that match the patient's primary concern (see the [Four Pillars](four-pillars.md)). Often the microbiome alone; add the proteomic blood and/or methylation test where the pillar warrants it.
2. **Interpret in context.** Read results alongside symptoms, history, and goals — never in isolation. Flag anything that needs a physician.
3. **Intervene (non-pharmacological first).** Apply targeted lifestyle changes — fibre and diet, sleep, stress, specific nutrition. These are exactly the levers that move the microbiome and methylation.[^fan2020][^moore2012][^scfa2023]
4. **Re-measure.** Re-test at a sensible interval (commonly every few months) to see whether the biomarkers moved, and adjust the plan.
5. **Coordinate care.** Route anything medical — diagnosis, prescriptions, or medication dosing — to the patient's physician. For patients on a GLP-1, share the information layer to support the prescriber's individualised plan.[^glp1micro2023]

!!! tip "So what?"
    The loop is the product. A single result is interesting; a baseline-and-recheck story that shows improvement is what changes behaviour and demonstrates value — for the patient, the practitioner, and us.

## Why it matters for Dayhoff / DHealth

Practitioners are a high-leverage channel: they see the patients, set the plans, and follow up. Meeting them where they work — GPs wanting a credible adjunct, naturopaths wanting objective evidence for lifestyle care — and giving them a clean, honest workflow makes the testing easy to adopt and easy to defend.

## Common questions

**"Will this replace the bloodwork I already order?"**
No. It adds a functional and modifiable layer and is an adjunct to standard care, not a replacement.[^quince2017][^metaomics2019]

**"Can a naturopath use this to treat disease?"**
The tests inform lifestyle interventions and tracking. Diagnosis and treatment of disease remain within the scope of appropriately licensed clinicians.

**"How often should we re-test?"**
Often every few months — long enough for lifestyle changes to register, short enough to keep the patient engaged. Match the cadence to the intervention.
