# GLP-1 Biomarkers: Microbiome & Blood Signals

!!! info "Page status"
    **Level:** Advanced &nbsp;·&nbsp; **Status:** :material-circle: Drafted &nbsp;·&nbsp; **Last reviewed:** 2026-06-14

> **Purpose:** The biomarkers that matter most for people on (or considering) a GLP-1 — what they are, which direction they tend to move with effective therapy, and what they signal — across the microbiome and the proteomic blood panel.

!!! warning "Important — informational, not dosing guidance"
    This page is **informational**. It is **not** a dosing protocol and must not be used to start, stop, or adjust any GLP-1 medication. Interpretation and every medication decision belong to the **prescriber**. Marker directions below describe *typical* patterns, not rules, and individual results require clinical context.

!!! abstract "In simple terms"
    GLP-1 medications work better for some people than others, and they can cause muscle loss if unmanaged. Certain gut microbes and blood proteins give an early read on **how well it's working** and **whether the body is handling it safely**. This page lists those signals so the care team can watch the right things over time.

## Key points

- **Gut microbes shape GLP-1 response.** Fibre-fermenting, SCFA-producing communities tend to support better outcomes; inflammatory, low-diversity communities tend to blunt them.[^scfa2023][^glp1micro2023]
- **Blood proteins track efficacy and safety.** Falling inflammation and metabolic markers generally signal a good response; a specific pattern can flag **muscle-wasting risk**.
- **The muscle-wasting signature matters most for safety** and is the clearest reason to involve the prescriber early.
- These are **monitoring signals**, not diagnoses or dosing instructions.

## A. Microbiome signals

From shotgun stool profiling, certain patterns associate with GLP-1 response (mechanisms below are supported by published reviews; specific strain associations are an active research area).

| Pattern | Microbiome signal | What it reflects |
|---|---|---|
| **Favourable response** | Higher baseline diversity; SCFA-producers (*Faecalibacterium prausnitzii*, *Roseburia*); *Bacteroides*; *Akkermansia muciniphila* | Fibre fermentation makes short-chain fatty acids (acetate, propionate, butyrate) that **trigger the body's own GLP-1 release** and improve insulin sensitivity — working with the drug.[^scfa2023][^glp1micro2023][^akkermansia2021] |
| **Blunted response / more GI side effects** | Low diversity; expansion of LPS-producing Gram-negatives (e.g. *Enterobacteriaceae*) | A leaky barrier lets bacterial LPS into circulation, driving "metabolic endotoxemia" and inflammation that can dampen efficacy and worsen GI symptoms.[^scfa2023][^glp1micro2023] |
| **Muscle-wasting risk** | Depletion of SCFA- and branched-chain-amino-acid–producing commensals under drug-induced caloric/protein restriction | Macronutrient deprivation plus low-grade inflammation (TNF-α, IL-6) accelerates lean-mass loss — a key safety concern with aggressive titration.[^scfa2023] |

!!! tip "So what?"
    A favourable microbiome is an asset to protect (fibre, diet); an inflammatory one is a target to improve. Either way, it gives the care team something concrete to act on alongside the medication.

## B. Proteomic blood panel

Grouped by what they tell you. **"Typical direction"** = the pattern usually seen with an *effective, well-tolerated* GLP-1 response; deviations are signals to interpret clinically, not act on automatically.

### Glycemic & metabolic efficacy
| Marker (also known as) | Typical direction | What it reflects |
|---|---|---|
| HbA1c | ↓ | 90-day average glucose; confirms improved glycemic control |
| Fasting glucose | ↓ | Acute glucose-dependent insulin/glucagon effect |
| FGF21 | context-dependent | Glucose/lipid homeostasis; a **sharp rise** is a muscle-stress flag (see watch list) |

### Cardiovascular & endothelial
| Marker (also known as) | Typical direction | What it reflects |
|---|---|---|
| hs-CRP | ↓ | Systemic inflammation; lower = reduced cardiovascular risk |
| ICAM1 | ↓ | Endothelial (blood-vessel) inflammation |
| MMP9 | ↓ | Arterial-plaque/tissue remodeling; stabilization |
| SELE (E-selectin) | ↓ | Leukocyte–endothelial adhesion |
| PTX3 (Pentraxin 3) | ↓ | Vascular inflammation / plaque vulnerability |
| VEGFA | monitor | Tissue repair/angiogenesis; high levels warrant eye monitoring (retinopathy) |

### Renal (kidney) safety
| Marker | Typical direction | What it reflects |
|---|---|---|
| eGFR / serum creatinine | stable→improved eGFR | Long-term kidney protection; an **acute creatinine spike / eGFR drop** is a flag for volume depletion |
| Urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio (UACR) | ↓ | Slowing of diabetic kidney disease |

### Hepatic (liver) health
| Marker | Typical direction | What it reflects |
|---|---|---|
| ALT / AST | ↓ | Reduced liver fat (MASLD); a paradoxical spike warrants evaluation |

### Systemic inflammation & adipose
| Marker (also known as) | Typical direction | What it reflects |
|---|---|---|
| IL6 | ↓ | Core metabolic-inflammation / insulin-sensitivity tracker |
| IL1B | ↓ | NLRP3 inflammasome activity in vascular linings |
| CCL2 (MCP-1) | ↓ | Macrophage recruitment into adipose/vascular tissue |
| CXCL10 (IP-10) | ↓ | Systemic low-grade inflammation |
| CHI3L1 (YKL-40) | ↓ | Insulin-resistance / fatty-liver inflammation |
| AGER (RAGE) | ↓ | Advanced-glycation stress and vascular injury |

### :material-alert: Muscle-atrophy & catabolic watch (the key safety signature)
A combined pattern is more informative than any single marker:

| Marker (also known as) | Watch signal | What it may indicate |
|---|---|---|
| FGF21 | **sharp ↑** | Skeletal-muscle mitochondrial stress / lean-tissue wasting |
| GDF2 (BMP9) | **↓** | Imbalanced growth signaling; sarcopenia risk |
| AGRP (Agouti-related peptide) | **excessively ↓** | Appetite over-suppression; risk of unmet protein/calorie floor |

!!! danger "Muscle-wasting is the signal to escalate"
    A rising **FGF21** together with falling **GDF2 (BMP9)** and over-suppressed **AGRP** suggests the body is losing lean muscle rather than just fat. This is **information to bring to the prescriber promptly** — typical responses (more dietary protein, resistance training, and reviewing the medication plan) are clinical decisions for them to make, not actions taken from this report.

## How to use it (with the care team)

Pair this with the [GP Care Plan](gp-care-plan.md) loop: baseline before or early in therapy, re-check on the prescriber's cadence (often every 1–3 months during titration), and watch the **efficacy** markers trend down while screening for the **muscle-wasting** signature. Route every interpretation and dosing decision to the prescriber.
