# The Four Pillars & Practical Use Cases

!!! info "Page status"
    **Level:** Intermediate &nbsp;·&nbsp; **Status:** :material-circle: Drafted &nbsp;·&nbsp; **Last reviewed:** 2026-06-14

> **Purpose:** Translate the science into concrete, honest use cases — the four health areas where microbiome (and complementary blood and methylation) testing is most useful, and how each helps with real concerns like GLP-1 support, Crohn's, colitis, and everyday gut issues.

!!! warning "Important — this is informational, not medical advice"
    The tests described here provide **informational, wellness, and research-context** insights. They are **not a diagnostic device** and do **not** diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent disease. They **complement** — they do not replace — a qualified clinician's judgment. Any decision about medication, including **GLP-1 dosing**, remains with the patient's prescriber. Frame every customer conversation accordingly.

## In one sentence

We focus on **four pillars** where the evidence linking the microbiome to health is strongest, and we are honest that these are where the data is solid — rather than claiming the microbiome predicts everything.[^knight2018]

## Key points

- The four pillars are **Metabolic health**, **Brain & mental health**, **Fatigue & wellness**, and **Gastrointestinal & allergy**.
- For each pillar, the **microbiome** test tells you the ecosystem and its function, the **proteomic blood** test adds a real-time systemic snapshot, and **methylation** tracks biological aging.
- A core advantage: microbiome and methylation are **modifiable with lifestyle**, so they support a *measure → intervene → re-measure* loop.[^fan2020][^moore2012]
- Use cases below (GLP-1, Crohn's, colitis, gut issues) are framed as **information that supports** clinical and lifestyle decisions, never as a substitute for them.

!!! abstract "In simple terms"
    Think of the body as having four "rooms" where gut microbes have the biggest, best-proven influence: how you burn energy, how your brain and mood work, how much energy and vitality you have, and how your gut and immune system behave. We test those four rooms well, help people make lifestyle changes, and then re-test to show whether things moved in the right direction.

## The four pillars

### 1. Metabolic health (weight, diabetes, GLP-1 support)
The gut microbiota is closely tied to metabolic health and disease, including obesity, type 2 diabetes, and fatty-liver and cardio-metabolic disease.[^fan2020] Specific patterns matter: for example, higher abundance of *Akkermansia* is associated with **lower** metabolic-syndrome risk in a dose-dependent way,[^akkermansia2021] and functional (short-chain-fatty-acid) capacity has been linked to protection in type 1 diabetes.[^t1d2018]

**GLP-1 connection.** Gut microbes and the hormone GLP-1 interact in both directions: microbial metabolites (short-chain fatty acids) stimulate the body's own GLP-1 secretion, microbiota composition shifts in obesity and type 2 diabetes, and reviews emphasise that GLP-1-based therapy should be **individualised**.[^glp1micro2023][^scfa2023]

!!! tip "So what? (GLP-1 use case)"
    For someone on a GLP-1 medication, microbiome and proteomic-blood markers can give an **information layer** on metabolic and nutritional status and how it changes over time. That supports a richer conversation between the patient and their prescriber about response and tolerability — it does **not** tell anyone what dose to take. Dosing stays with the prescriber.

### 2. Brain & mental health
A large and growing literature links the gut microbiome to neurological and neuropsychiatric conditions through the gut–brain axis.[^sorboni2022] Mechanistically, microbial metabolites can influence brain and behaviour, including through epigenetic regulation of host genes.[^caspani2019]

!!! tip "So what?"
    Mood, focus, and brain health are among the concerns people care about most day to day. The microbiome offers an objective, trackable angle on the gut–brain connection to support lifestyle conversations.

### 3. Fatigue & wellness (energy, healthy aging)
Lower microbial diversity is associated with frailty,[^frailty2016] and microbiome profiles can even be used to estimate biological age, complementing methylation-based aging measures.[^microbiomeage2025][^moore2012]

!!! tip "So what?"
    "I'm tired all the time" is one of the most common reasons people seek help. Diversity, functional capacity, and biological-age markers give something concrete to measure and improve.

### 4. Gastrointestinal & allergy (Crohn's, colitis, gut issues)
This is the microbiome's home turf. Shotgun sequencing resolves organisms to species/strain level and profiles their function.[^quince2017][^johnson2019] Concrete examples:

- **Crohn's disease:** *Ruminococcus gnavus*, a gut microbe enriched in Crohn's, produces an inflammatory polysaccharide that drives a pro-inflammatory immune response.[^henke2019]
- **Ulcerative colitis:** ecological/network analyses have identified specific microbial species relevant to UC diagnosis and management.[^uc2021]
- **General gut health:** short-chain fatty acids from fibre fermentation support the gut barrier and dampen inflammation,[^scfa2023][^alsharairi2023] and large cohorts link gut-metagenome features to many health conditions.[^gutmeta2022]

!!! tip "So what? (Crohn's / colitis / gut)"
    For gut complaints, the microbiome can characterise *which* organisms are present at strain level **and** what they are doing (e.g. inflammatory taxa vs. SCFA-producers). That turns a vague "gut issue" into something specific to target with diet and lifestyle, and to re-measure over time.

## Staying honest

A deliberate part of our positioning: we concentrate on these four pillars **because the data supports them**. We avoid the common over-claim that the microbiome can predict almost anything — careful, best-practice analysis matters.[^knight2018]

## Why it matters for Dayhoff / DHealth

The four pillars convert abstract science into the conversations customers actually have: "Will this help my weight / my gut / my energy / my brain?" Leading with the pillar that matches the customer's concern — then explaining what each test contributes — is more credible and more useful than a feature list, and keeps us within honest, defensible claims.

## Common questions

**"Can this tell me how to dose my GLP-1?"**
No. It provides information about metabolic and nutritional status over time that supports the conversation with your prescriber. Dosing decisions are medical and stay with the prescriber.

**"Does it diagnose Crohn's or colitis?"**
No. It characterises the gut ecosystem and its function, which can be informative alongside — not instead of — clinical evaluation and diagnosis by a physician.[^henke2019][^uc2021]

**"Why only four areas?"**
Because that is where the evidence is strongest. We would rather be right and useful than over-promise.[^knight2018]
