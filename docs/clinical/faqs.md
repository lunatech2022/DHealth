# Sales FAQs & Objection Handling

!!! info "Page status"
    **Level:** Beginner &nbsp;·&nbsp; **Status:** :material-circle: Drafted &nbsp;·&nbsp; **Last reviewed:** 2026-06-14

> **Purpose:** Give the team short, accurate, referenced answers to the questions customers ask most, so you can respond with confidence and credibility.

## In one sentence

These are the common questions about microbiome and biological-age testing, each with a clear, science-backed answer you can use almost word-for-word.

## Key points

- Lead with the simple answer, then add detail only if the customer wants it.
- "Microbiome testing" means reading which microbes live in a sample, usually the gut.
- 16S and shotgun are two methods with different resolution and cost.
- There is no single "ideal" microbiome, but there are healthy patterns.
- Results give insight and a baseline to track, not a medical diagnosis.

## The detail

**What is microbiome testing?** It is a way to read which microbes (mostly bacteria) live in a sample, typically the gut. The output describes the community: which groups are present and in what relative amounts. A healthy gut microbiome is generally diverse and balanced, shaped by diet, environment, and lifestyle.[^rinninella2019]

**What's the difference between 16S and shotgun?** Both sequence DNA, but differently. **16S** reads a single marker gene shared by bacteria; it is cost-effective and identifies microbes well to the genus level, sometimes to species.[^johnson2019] **Shotgun** sequences all the DNA in a sample, giving finer taxonomic detail and insight into functional potential, the genes and pathways a community carries.[^quince2017] Shorthand: 16S tells you *who is roughly there*; shotgun tells you *who is there and what they can do*.

**Is there an ideal microbiome?** No single template fits everyone. Healthy microbiomes share features like diversity and balance, but composition varies widely between healthy people.[^rinninella2019] The honest framing is "patterns associated with health," not "one perfect result."

**What can results actually tell me?** They give a baseline and insight, especially valuable to track over time as someone changes diet or habits. The microbiome is genuinely central to human metabolic health,[^fan2020] which is why the insight matters, but for most consumer testing the results are not a diagnosis. Be clear and confident on this point; it builds trust.

**What is biological / methylation age?** Biological age estimates how your body is aging, which can differ from your calendar age. One major approach reads **DNA methylation**, chemical marks added to DNA that help switch genes on or off, a foundational biological mechanism.[^moore2012] Patterns of these marks underpin many biological-age "clocks." It is a molecular snapshot of aging, not a prediction of lifespan.

!!! tip "So what?"
    The strongest sales answer is an honest one. Saying "this gives you insight and a baseline to track, not a diagnosis" sets correct expectations and makes everything else you say more believable.

## Why it matters for Dayhoff / DHealth

Consistent, accurate answers protect credibility and shorten sales cycles. When the whole team frames the science the same honest way, customers trust the product and you avoid overpromising, which is the fastest way to lose a deal in this field.

## Common questions

- **Will my results change?** Yes, the microbiome shifts with diet, environment, and time, which is exactly why tracking is useful.
- **Can I compare my result to a friend's?** Loosely. Healthy people differ a lot, so focus on your own trend rather than head-to-head comparison.
- **Is methylation age the same as my microbiome age?** No, they are different molecular signals; both are estimates of aging from different data.
