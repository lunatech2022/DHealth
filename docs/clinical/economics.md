# The Economics: Cost Savings for Payers, Employers & Stop-Loss

!!! info "Page status"
    **Level:** Intermediate &nbsp;·&nbsp; **Status:** :material-circle: Drafted &nbsp;·&nbsp; **Last reviewed:** 2026-06-14

> **Purpose:** Show the documented cost burden of the conditions our four pillars target, and provide an **illustrative, assumption-driven** framework for how early detection and lifestyle intervention *could* reduce spend for insurers, employers, and stop-loss carriers.

!!! danger "Read this first — this is an illustrative model, not a guarantee"
    The **cost-of-disease figures below are real and cited.** The **savings scenarios are illustrative** and depend on assumptions (engagement, effect size, time horizon) that **have not yet been validated for our program**. Nothing here is a promised return, a guarantee of clinical outcomes, or actuarial/financial advice. Treat it as a framework for a conversation, with each assumption made explicit so a partner can substitute their own numbers.

## In one sentence

The conditions we focus on cost the system **hundreds of billions per year**; because the microbiome and methylation are **modifiable**, the thesis is that earlier detection plus lifestyle intervention could **avoid or delay** a portion of that spend — and even a small portion is large.[^fan2020][^moore2012]

## Key points

- The four target areas are among the most expensive in healthcare — diabetes alone was **$412.9B** in the US in 2022.[^parker2024]
- Per-patient costs are high and recurring, so **delaying or avoiding** progression compounds over a lifetime.
- Different stakeholders capture savings differently: **insurers** via medical loss, **employers** via productivity and premiums, **stop-loss** via fewer catastrophic claims.
- The model is **transparent and conservative**: substitute your own engagement and effect-size assumptions.

## The documented cost burden (real, cited)

| Condition (pillar) | Headline US cost | Per-person figure |
|---|---|---|
| **Type 2 diabetes / metabolic** | **$412.9B/yr** total (2022)[^parker2024] | ~**$12,022/yr** attributable per person; people with diabetes have 2.6× the medical spend[^parker2024] |
| **IBD (Crohn's & colitis)** | Rising US claims burden[^park2020] | ~**$22,987/yr** direct cost per patient vs ~$6,956 for non-IBD controls (>3×); ~**$26,555** in the first year after diagnosis[^park2020] |
| **Obesity / cardiometabolic** | **$149.4B/yr** national[^kim2016] | ~**$1,901/yr** in added medical spend per adult with obesity[^kim2016] |
| **Mental health (MDD)** | **$326.2B/yr** incremental burden (2018)[^greenberg2021] | Majority is **workplace** cost (absenteeism/presenteeism)[^greenberg2021] |

These are the claims the program aims to help **prevent, delay, or better manage**.

## The prevention thesis

Three things make a cost-avoidance argument plausible (note: *plausible*, pending outcome validation):

1. **The targets are expensive and progressive.** Diabetes, IBD, obesity, and depression accrue cost year after year, so moving the curve earlier compounds.[^parker2024][^park2020]
2. **The biomarkers are modifiable.** Unlike a fixed genome, the microbiome and methylation respond to diet, sleep, and lifestyle — the substrate for non-pharmacological intervention and tracking.[^fan2020][^moore2012][^scfa2023]
3. **Early signals exist.** Microbiome and functional markers are associated with metabolic, gut, and mental-health states, offering a potential earlier read than waiting for a clinical event.[^gutmeta2022][^glp1micro2023]

## An illustrative savings framework

A transparent worked example. **Every number marked "assumption" is illustrative**, for the partner to replace.

- Take a person with type 2 diabetes incurring ~**$12,022/yr** in attributable cost.[^parker2024]
- Over a 20-year horizon (assumption), that is ~**$240,000** undiscounted lifetime attributable spend.
- *If* a program helped **delay onset or progression** or improve management enough to avoid even **10%** of that (assumption, not a measured effect), that is ~**$24,000** avoided **per patient** over the horizon.
- Across a population, multiply by the number of at-risk members engaged (assumption) and net of program cost (a variable you set).

The same arithmetic applies to IBD (>$22,987/yr per patient) and obesity-linked cost. The point is structural: **high recurring per-patient cost × any meaningful avoided fraction × many members = large absolute numbers** — but the avoided fraction must be earned with real outcome data.

## Who benefits, and how

=== "Insurance providers (payers)"

    Savings show up as **reduced medical loss ratio**: fewer ED visits, hospitalizations, and complications among engaged members in the four pillars. Because diabetes care alone is ~1 in 4 US healthcare dollars,[^parker2024] even modest avoided progression is material. Best framed as a **value-based / shared-savings** pilot with agreed outcome metrics.

=== "Employers"

    Employers carry both **direct medical premiums and indirect productivity loss**. Diabetes drove ~$106.3B in indirect costs (disability, presenteeism, lost productivity),[^parker2024] and the majority of the MDD burden is **workplace** cost.[^greenberg2021] A program that improves metabolic and mental-health trajectories can reduce **absenteeism and presenteeism** as well as claims.

=== "Stop-loss / reinsurance"

    Stop-loss exposure is driven by **catastrophic, high-cost claimants**. IBD costs spike around diagnosis and relapse and are driven by biologics, ED use, and complications;[^park2020] diabetes complications are similarly back-loaded. Earlier identification and better management aim to **keep members from crossing into catastrophic-claim territory**, reducing the tail risk stop-loss carriers price against.

!!! tip "So what? (how to use this with a partner)"
    Lead with the **real burden figures**, then offer the **framework** with the partner's own assumptions, and propose a **measured pilot** with agreed outcome metrics. Promise the rigor, not a specific ROI — that is both more honest and more credible.

## Why it matters for Dayhoff / DHealth

Payers, employers, and stop-loss carriers buy **avoided cost**, not science. Translating the four pillars into their financial language — medical loss, productivity, tail risk — is how the clinical story becomes a commercial one, provided we stay disciplined about what is proven versus projected.

## Common questions

**"Can we promise a specific ROI or savings percentage?"**
No. The disease costs are real and citable; the savings are illustrative until validated in a measured program. Offer a pilot with agreed metrics, not a guaranteed number.

**"Why will earlier detection save money?"**
Because these conditions are expensive and progressive, and the relevant biomarkers are modifiable — so acting earlier *can* avoid downstream cost. The size of the effect must be measured.[^fan2020][^parker2024]
