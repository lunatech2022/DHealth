# Competitive Landscape

!!! info "Page status"
    **Level:** Intermediate &nbsp;·&nbsp; **Status:** :material-circle: Drafted &nbsp;·&nbsp; **Last reviewed:** 2026-06-14

> **Purpose:** Give the team a generic, educational map of the microbiome-testing market by method category, so you understand how the *method* a product uses shapes what it can credibly claim.

## In one sentence

The microbiome-testing market splits into broad categories defined by the sequencing method behind them, and that method choice, more than branding, determines the resolution and the kinds of claims a product can honestly make.

## Key points

- Think in **categories and methods**, not named companies.
- Three broad categories: **consumer 16S kits**, **clinical / shotgun metagenomics**, and **research-grade** offerings.
- Method capability sets the ceiling on resolution: how deeply a test can identify microbes and their functions.
- Higher-resolution methods enable stronger, more specific claims, usually at higher cost and complexity.
- This page is general and educational. Detailed internal competitive analysis is maintained separately in internal documents.

## The detail

The simplest way to understand the market is by the **method** each product is built on. Two dominant DNA-sequencing approaches anchor the categories.

**16S rRNA gene sequencing** reads one marker gene shared by bacteria. It is cost-effective and well established, but its resolution is generally limited: it identifies microbes reliably to the genus level and only sometimes to species or strain.[^johnson2019] This is the engine behind most **consumer kits**, where the goal is an accessible overview of community composition rather than deep functional detail.

**Shotgun metagenomics** sequences all DNA in a sample rather than one gene. This gives finer taxonomic resolution and, critically, insight into *functional* potential: the genes and pathways a community carries.[^quince2017] It is the basis for more **clinical and research-grade** offerings. A specialised, high-resolution relative of this approach, clinical metagenomic next-generation sequencing, is used for unbiased pathogen detection in diagnostics.[^gu2019] Foundational large-scale efforts such as the Human Microbiome Project mapped the structure, function, and diversity of the healthy human microbiome and set reference points the whole field still builds on.[^hmp2012]

**How method drives claims.** The principle to internalise is that *a product cannot claim more resolution than its method provides*. A 16S-based consumer kit can responsibly describe community balance and diversity at the genus level. A shotgun-based product can go further into species, strain, and functional pathways, which supports more specific statements. Mismatching the two, making species- or function-level claims off a method that cannot resolve them, is where products lose scientific credibility.

| Category | Typical method | Resolution | Claims it can support |
|---|---|---|---|
| Consumer kits | 16S rRNA | Genus, sometimes species[^johnson2019] | Community overview, diversity, composition |
| Clinical / shotgun | Shotgun metagenomics | Species, strain, function[^quince2017] | Functional potential, finer taxonomy, pathogen detection[^gu2019] |
| Research-grade | Shotgun + bespoke pipelines | Highest, study-specific | Discovery, hypothesis testing, reference building[^hmp2012] |

!!! tip "So what?"
    When you hear a competitor's claim, ask: *what method produces this?* If a product promises strain-level or functional insight, it needs shotgun-grade data behind it. Method is the honest yardstick for credibility, and a clean way to frame value without naming anyone.

## Why it matters for Dayhoff / DHealth

Framing the market by method keeps sales conversations accurate and defensible. You can explain trade-offs (cost vs. resolution) and set expectations without overpromising or disparaging competitors. For specifics on how we position relative to others, refer to internal documents rather than this public page.

## Common questions

- **Why are some tests cheaper?** Usually because they use 16S, which is lower-cost but lower-resolution than shotgun methods.
- **Is more resolution always better?** Not always. The right method depends on the question; a community overview may be all a wellness use case needs.
