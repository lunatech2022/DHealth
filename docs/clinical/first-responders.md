# First Responders: Why Testing Helps a High-Risk Group

!!! info "Page status"
    **Level:** Intermediate &nbsp;·&nbsp; **Status:** :material-circle: Drafted &nbsp;·&nbsp; **Last reviewed:** 2026-06-14

> **Purpose:** Explain, from published research, why first responders (firefighters, police, EMS, and similar shift-working, high-stress roles) are a high-risk group that stands to benefit from microbiome, methylation, and proteomic-blood testing.

!!! warning "Important — informational, not medical advice"
    This page summarises published research on first-responder health and how the relevant biomarkers connect to it. The tests are **informational** and an **adjunct** to clinical care; they do not diagnose, treat, or cure disease. Occupational-health and medical decisions remain with qualified clinicians.

## In one sentence

First responders carry **elevated, partly modifiable** health risks — cardiac and metabolic strain, shift-work disruption, and trauma-related mental-health burden — that map directly onto the four areas these tests measure best.[^smith2011][^touitou2025][^mackay2024]

## Key points

- The leading cause of line-of-duty death among firefighters is **sudden cardiac event** (~45% of duty deaths), and many carry modifiable cardiovascular risk factors.[^smith2011]
- **Shift work and circadian disruption** are linked to gut dysbiosis, inflammation, and metabolic dysfunction.[^touitou2025]
- **Trauma exposure and PTSD** are common in this population, and the gut–brain axis is increasingly implicated in stress and PTSD.[^mackay2024][^sorboni2022]
- Because these risks are **modifiable and trackable**, first responders are a natural fit for a baseline-and-recheck model.

!!! abstract "In simple terms"
    The job is hard on the body: broken sleep, high stress, intense physical demands, and trauma. All four of those show up in the gut, in metabolism, and in markers of aging — exactly what these tests read. So we can take a baseline, support healthier routines, and re-check whether things are improving.

## Why first responders are a high-risk group

- **Cardiac and metabolic strain.** Firefighting demands high aerobic and anaerobic capacity, yet a substantial share of firefighters are overweight and carry one or more modifiable cardiovascular risk factors; sudden cardiac event is the leading cause of on-duty death.[^smith2011]
- **Shift work and circadian disruption.** Irregular hours, night shifts, and light exposure misalign the body clock. This circadian disruption is associated with **gut dysbiosis, inflammation, and metabolic dysfunction**, via bidirectional clock–microbiome interactions.[^touitou2025]
- **Trauma and mental health.** Repeated exposure to critical incidents raises the burden of stress-related conditions. Research increasingly links the **gut–brain axis and microbiome** to anxiety and PTSD.[^mackay2024][^sorboni2022]
- **Cumulative wear.** The combination accelerates the kind of metabolic and inflammatory load that biological-age and diversity markers can track.[^frailty2016][^microbiomeage2025]

## How the tests help (mapped to the four pillars)

- **Metabolic health.** Track metabolic and nutritional status given the cardiac/metabolic risk profile, supporting diet and fitness plans.[^fan2020][^smith2011]
- **Gastrointestinal.** Shift-work dysbiosis and inflammation are readable in the microbiome; functional (SCFA) capacity gives targets for diet.[^touitou2025][^scfa2023]
- **Brain & mental health.** The gut–brain axis offers an objective, trackable angle alongside mental-health support for trauma-exposed personnel.[^mackay2024][^sorboni2022]
- **Fatigue & wellness / readiness.** Diversity, functional output, and biological-age markers speak to recovery and overall resilience in a physically demanding role.[^frailty2016][^microbiomeage2025][^athletemicro2022]

!!! tip "So what?"
    For a department or agency, this is a way to put **objective, modifiable biomarkers** behind wellness and readiness programs — baseline the team, support lifestyle change, and re-check. Keep it informational and coordinate any clinical findings with occupational-health and medical providers.

## Practical use

Apply the standard loop (see the [GP Care Plan](gp-care-plan.md)): baseline the relevant pillar(s), interpret in context, intervene with lifestyle (sleep hygiene around shifts, diet, stress, fitness), re-measure on a sensible cadence, and route anything clinical to the appropriate provider. Aggregate, de-identified trends can also inform program-level wellness decisions — with appropriate consent and privacy safeguards.

## Why it matters for Dayhoff / DHealth

First responders combine **high, documented risk** with a **strong wellness-program culture** and group purchasing. Meeting that with research-grounded, modifiable biomarkers — framed honestly as informational — is a credible, high-impact application of the four pillars.

## Common questions

**"Is this a medical screening program?"**
No. It is an informational wellness and tracking tool that complements — does not replace — occupational-health and medical care.

**"Which risks does it actually speak to?"**
Cardiac/metabolic strain, shift-work-related gut and metabolic disruption, and trauma/mental-health load via the gut–brain axis — all documented in this population.[^smith2011][^touitou2025][^mackay2024]
