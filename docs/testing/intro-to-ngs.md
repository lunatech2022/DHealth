# Introduction to NGS (Next-Generation Sequencing)

!!! info "Page status"
    **Level:** Beginner &nbsp;·&nbsp; **Status:** :material-circle: Drafted &nbsp;·&nbsp; **Last reviewed:** 2026-06-14

> **Purpose:** Give anyone — including non-scientists — a clear, repeatable explanation of what sequencing is, what makes it "next-generation," and why it powers modern microbiome and pathogen testing.

## In one sentence

Next-generation sequencing reads the DNA (and RNA) in a sample at massive scale and low cost, letting us identify the microbes present without having to grow them first.

## Key points

- **Sequencing = reading the genetic code.** It spells out the order of the letters (A, C, G, T) in DNA.
- **"Next-generation" means high-throughput.** Instead of reading one piece at a time, NGS reads millions of fragments in parallel.
- **Cost has collapsed.** Sequencing is dramatically cheaper than it was at the time of the Human Genome Project.[^goodwin2016]
- **Two broad flavors:** short-read (high accuracy, high volume) and long-read (better for complex regions).[^goodwin2016]
- **It underpins microbiome and pathogen testing** because nearly every organism carries DNA or RNA we can read.[^gu2019]

## The detail

**Start simple.** Every living thing carries instructions written in DNA, a long string of four chemical "letters." Sequencing is the act of reading that string. If you can read the DNA in a sample, you can work out *which* organisms are present, because different microbes have different genetic sequences.

**What "next-generation" adds.** Early sequencing read one fragment of DNA at a time, which was slow and expensive. Next-generation sequencing changed the economics by reading enormous numbers of DNA fragments in parallel. Since the Human Genome Project completed in 2003, the cost to sequence a given amount of DNA has fallen dramatically, which is what turned sequencing from a rare research tool into something usable in routine testing.[^goodwin2016]

**Short-read vs long-read.** NGS comes in two broad families. *Short-read* approaches generate very large numbers of short, highly accurate fragments and are the workhorse of high-throughput testing. *Long-read* approaches produce much longer stretches of sequence, which helps resolve structurally complex or repetitive regions that short reads struggle with.[^goodwin2016] Neither is universally "better"; they trade off accuracy, length, throughput, and cost.

**Why this matters for testing.** Nearly all pathogens — and microbiome members — contain DNA or RNA.[^gu2019] That means a single untargeted sequencing run can, in principle, detect organisms that conventional, culture-based diagnostics miss. Clinical metagenomic NGS is increasingly used precisely where standard methods have limitations, because it does not require knowing in advance what you are looking for.[^gu2019]

!!! tip "So what?"
    Because NGS reads genetic material directly, it can identify microbes that won't grow in a dish and pathogens that targeted tests aren't designed to catch. Combined with the collapse in cost, this is what makes comprehensive microbiome and diagnostic testing practical at scale today.[^goodwin2016] [^gu2019]

## Common questions

**Q: Do we have to grow the microbes first, like a lab culture?**
No. NGS reads DNA/RNA directly from the sample, so it can detect organisms that are difficult or impossible to culture.[^gu2019]

**Q: Is newer "long-read" sequencing always better?**
Not always. Long reads help with complex regions, but short-read sequencing offers very high accuracy and throughput. The right choice depends on the question.[^goodwin2016]

**Q: Why is sequencing affordable now when it used to cost a fortune?**
Throughput went up and cost per unit of sequence fell sharply after the Human Genome Project era.[^goodwin2016]
